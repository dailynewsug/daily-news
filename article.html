Government Unveils Landmark Infrastructure Plan Worth Billions in Historic Budget Session

The sweeping development plan will see roads, bridges, hospitals and public utilities upgraded across the country over the next five years in what analysts are calling the boldest economic move in a decade.

By John Doe Political Correspondent
Friday 15 May 2026

The parliament building where the historic budget session took place. Photograph: Daily News

The government has announced a sweeping infrastructure development plan that will see roads, bridges, hospitals and public utilities upgraded across the country over the next five years. The announcement, made during a historic parliamentary budget session on Thursday, has been described by economists as the most ambitious public investment programme in the country's history.

Speaking to a packed parliament, the Finance Minister outlined plans to allocate funds across five key sectors — transport, healthcare, education, energy and water — with the bulk of investment directed at rural communities that have historically been underserved by government spending.

"This is not just a budget. This is a statement of intent. We are investing in the future of every citizen, in every corner of this country." — Finance Minister

The plan has been broadly welcomed by business leaders and civil society organisations, though opposition lawmakers have raised questions about how the spending will be financed and whether adequate oversight mechanisms are in place to prevent corruption.

What the plan includes

According to documents released by the treasury, the infrastructure plan will fund the construction of over 2,000 kilometres of new tarmac roads, the rehabilitation of 500 existing road networks, and the building of 150 new health centres in underserved districts. Additionally, the plan includes the electrification of 1,200 villages currently without access to the national grid.

Education infrastructure will also receive a significant boost, with 300 new school buildings planned across the country and a major expansion of university facilities in three cities. Broadband internet connectivity is also included as part of the digital infrastructure component, aiming to connect all district headquarters by the end of 2027.

Reactions from economists

Leading economists have largely praised the ambition of the plan, though many have urged caution about implementation. Dr. Sarah Namukasa of the National Economic Research Institute described the announcement as "genuinely transformative if delivered", while warning that past infrastructure programmes have fallen short due to procurement delays and contractor underperformance.

The government has stated that it will introduce new public procurement reforms alongside the infrastructure plan, including mandatory performance bonds for all contractors and quarterly public reporting on project progress. An independent oversight board, comprising representatives from civil society, the private sector and academia, will be established within 90 days.

Opposition response

The main opposition party has called the plan "welcome but long overdue", while questioning the government's track record on delivery. Shadow Finance Minister Robert Okello told parliament that his party would support the plan in principle but would scrutinise every line of expenditure. "We have heard grand promises before," he said. "What matters is what gets built."

The plan is expected to be formally approved by parliament within the next three weeks, after which the government says work on priority road projects will begin immediately.
